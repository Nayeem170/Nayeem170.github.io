Free Domain Email That Lands in the Inbox

You own a domain but no mail server, no mailbox, and no budget. You want you@yourdomain.com that receives mail and sends mail Gmail and Outlook don't quietly file under spam. That is free, and it takes about an hour.

📋 What you need first

A domain, and any email account you already use. That's the whole list.

🎯 What you'll have at the end

Mail to your domain arriving in your normal inbox, and replies going out as your domain with no "via gmail.com" tag attached.

I'll use example.com and hello@example.com throughout. Swap in your own.

🚫 Why not just run a mail server?

Receiving mail is easy. Sending it is hard, and the reason is reputation, not software.

Big providers keep a score on every server on the internet: has it sent mail before, does it send spam, does it look real? A brand new $5 server scores badly on all three. It has no history, and it sits in a block of addresses Microsoft and Google already distrust. So your first email to an Outlook.com address goes to junk, and there is no support ticket that fixes it. Reputation is earned over months.

So don't fight it. Let someone else receive, let someone else's trusted servers send, and keep your domain name on the front of both.

📥 Step 1: receiving mail

Move your domain's nameservers to Cloudflare (the free plan is fine), then open your domain and click Email → Email Routing → Get started.

Cloudflare writes three MX records for you. An MX record is just a signpost in DNS saying "mail for this domain goes here":

example.com.  MX  52  route1.mx.cloudflare.net.   ; priorities are generated per domain
example.com.  MX  98  route2.mx.cloudflare.net.   ; and mean nothing - use your dashboard's,
example.com.  MX  91  route3.mx.cloudflare.net.   ; not these

Then go to Routes → Create address:

Custom address:  hello              -> becomes hello@example.com
Action:          Send to an email
Destination:     your-real-mailbox@gmail.com

That box only wants the part before the @, because Cloudflare already knows the domain. Click the verification link that lands in your mailbox and receiving is done, free, permanently.

Picking the address: avoid info@, admin@ and webmaster@, which have been on spammers' scraped lists since the 90s. And create the two or three addresses you actually use rather than a catch-all, or every random guess at your domain lands in your inbox.

What you don't get: no mailbox on the domain, no IMAP, no webmail, no sending, and messages above roughly 25 MB will bounce.

📤 Step 2: getting permission to send

Anyone can type hello@example.com in a From field. That is why receivers ignore it and check who actually delivered the message instead. A server with no authority over your domain looks exactly like forgery, because that is what forgery is.

So you need a service that sends from trusted servers and can prove it speaks for you. Brevo's free tier does both: 300 emails a day, no card required.

Sign up, then Settings → Domains → Add domain. Brevo gives you DNS records to add:

; proves the domain is yours
example.com.          TXT    "brevo-code:<code>"

; the signing keys  (older accounts get mail._domainkey / mail2._domainkey)
brevo1._domainkey     CNAME  b1.<code>.dkim.brevo.com.
brevo2._domainkey     CNAME  b2.<code>.dkim.brevo.com.

Add them in Cloudflare with the cloud icon grey, never orange. Then Settings → SMTP & API gives you the credentials your mail app needs: 

Server:    smtp-relay.brevo.com
Port:      587
Username:  <code>@smtp-brevo.com
Password:  <generated SMTP key>
⚠️ That key is a password. Whoever holds it can send mail signed as your domain. Keep it out of your git repo.

✉️ Step 3: sending from the mailbox you already use

In Gmail: Settings → Accounts and Import → Send mail as → Add another email address.

Field Value
Email address hello@example.com
Treat as an alias unticked
SMTP Server / Port smtp-relay.brevo.com, 587
Username / Password your relay login and key
Security Secured connection using TLS

Gmail sends a verification code to hello@example.com. Cloudflare forwards it into the inbox you're already sitting in. Paste it back and you're done. The same fields work in Thunderbird, Apple Mail or mutt - Gmail is just the common case.

⚠️ Untick "Treat as an alias", or Gmail treats the new address as part of your personal account and your real address can show up when people reply.

🔐 Step 4: the three records that keep you out of spam

Everything so far was plumbing. This part decides inbox versus junk.

Think of an email like a letter. The letterhead is the From: line your reader sees. Anyone can print letterhead, so receivers check the wax seal on the envelope instead. Three DNS records make that seal work.

SPF - the guest list

SPF is one line in DNS naming which services are allowed to send as your domain. The receiver looks it up and checks whether the server that just delivered your message is on the list.

You get exactly one SPF record. Publishing two isn't two lists, it's a permanent error, and receivers trust that less than no record at all. So merge every service into one line:

example.com.  TXT  "v=spf1 include:_spf.mx.cloudflare.net include:spf.brevo.com ~all"
⚠️ It's spf.brevo.com, not _spf.brevo.com - Google and Cloudflare use the underscore, Brevo doesn't. Get it wrong and nothing visibly breaks: your mail still sends, the record just quietly means nothing. Same silent failure if you list more than ten services.

DKIM - the wax seal

DKIM stamps every message with a signature only someone holding your domain's key can make. The matching public key sits in your DNS, so any receiver can check it. Add the CNAME records your relay gave you and it stamps everything on the way out.

Here is the part worth reading twice. Every email carries two "from" addresses, and only one of them is on your screen:

sequenceDiagram
    participant You as Your mailbox
    participant Brevo as Brevo relay
    participant Rx as Recipient

    You->>Brevo: compose, From: hello@example.com
    Note over Brevo: hidden address (for bounces)
= bounce@brevo.com
visible address (letterhead)
= hello@example.com
    Brevo->>Rx: stamped with your seal, d=example.com
    Rx->>Rx: SPF checks the HIDDEN one, passes for brevo.com
    Rx->>Rx: DKIM checks the VISIBLE one, passes for example.com
    Note over Rx: Only the visible one counts.
DKIM is what carries you.

SPF passes, but it passes for Brevo's domain. It says nothing about the letterhead your recipient actually reads. DKIM is the one that lines up.

Key point: your mail lands because of DKIM. Break those CNAMEs and everything fails, however tidy your SPF record is.

This is also why you should ignore the many guides that tell you to put smtp.gmail.com in that SMTP box instead. Gmail can only stamp its own seal:

Mismatched, so recipients get the "via gmail.com" tag you were trying to remove in the first place. It looks fine at first because nothing is enforcing the rules yet - see the next record - and then breaks later, when you turn enforcement on.

DMARC - your policy

DMARC ties the other two together and tells receivers what to do when a message claiming to be you passes neither.

_dmarc.example.com.  TXT  "v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@example.com"

p=none means "monitor only": nothing gets blocked, but reports start arriving at the rua address showing who sends as your domain and whether it checks out. Publish it on day one, read the reports for a few weeks, then tighten.

Phase Policy Effect
Day 1 p=none Monitor only, nothing blocked
After 2-4 weeks of clean reports p=quarantine Failing mail goes to spam
Steady state p=reject Failing mail is refused outright

That last step is where a broken setup finally bites: with Gmail's SMTP and nothing aligned, turning on enforcement sends your own mail to spam. Since Google and Yahoo tightened their rules in 2024, none of this is optional polish anymore.

cf2024-1._domainkey   TXT    <Cloudflare's key, auto-generated>
⚠️ Every mail record is grey cloud, DNS only. The orange cloud is for websites and will break your mail. Duplicate SPF or DMARC records break it silently.

🔍 Step 5: check it before you hand the address out

dig +short TXT example.com          # should return exactly one SPF line
dig +short TXT _dmarc.example.com   # should return exactly one DMARC line
dig +short CNAME brevo1._domainkey.example.com

Send a message from your new address to mail-tester.com, which scores it out of 10 and names whatever failed. Below 8, stop and fix. Mail the address from an unrelated account too, to confirm forwarding works the other way.

Then open your test message in Gmail and click ⋮ → Show original:

SPF:   PASS with domain brevo.com        <- fine, expected
DKIM:  PASS with domain example.com      <- THIS is the one that matters
DMARC: PASS
The one check that matters: if the DKIM line shows the relay's domain instead of yours, your mail is passing by luck, not configuration. Go back and fix the CNAMEs.

💰 Cost, limits and alternatives

$0 a month. The 300/day limit counts recipients rather than messages, so one email to five people costs five. For a contact address you will never come close.

Instead of Options
Cloudflare receiving Forward Email (open source, no volume cap, works with any DNS host), ImprovMX
Brevo sending Mailjet 6,000/mo but 200/day, MailerSend 3,000/mo, SMTP2GO 1,000/mo, Resend, Amazon SES (~$0.10 per thousand, sandboxed at first), Postmark (paid, best deliverability)
All three at once Zoho Mail's free tier hosts the domain itself - five users, no relay needed, but no IMAP
A real mailbox on the domain Fastmail, Proton, Migadu, Purelymail, a few dollars a month

Free tiers move constantly, so check the pricing page rather than this table.

You outgrow this setup past 300 sends a day, or when you need IMAP, shared inboxes, or to email a mailing list - that last one needs a proper marketing service that handles unsubscribes, not a relay. And if the address feeds a contact form, you are handling other people's personal data: GDPR wants a privacy notice, a retention period you actually enforce, and deletion requests answered within 30 days.

✅ Summary

📥 Inbound

Cloudflare Email Routing publishes MX for your domain and forwards to a mailbox you already own

📤 Outbound

A free SMTP relay sends from trusted servers and stamps the message as your domain

🔐 Identity

One merged SPF record, your relay's DKIM keys, and a DMARC record starting at p=none

🔍 Verify

mail-tester above 8, and Show original showing DKIM signed by your own domain

Key Takeaway: through a shared relay, DKIM is what carries you, not SPF. The envelope sender belongs to the relay, so SPF passes for the relay's domain and only DKIM lines up with the address your recipient actually reads. Get the seal right and everything else follows.
